Project Overview

Electromyography (EMG) is the technique of measuring the small electrical signals produced by muscle contractions. These signals, typically in the range of [50µV to 5mV], require significant amplification and careful filtering to be useful for detection or control applications. EMG circuits are used in prosthetics, biomedical research, human-machine interfaces, and clinical diagnostics.

For my MIT 6.2040 Analog Electronics Laboratory final project, my partner and I designed a complete, fully-analog EMG detection system. The circuit takes raw biopotential signals from surface electrodes, amplifies and filters them, and produces a clean signal that triggers visual feedback (LEDs) when muscle activity is detected.

My contribution: I designed and built the signal acquisition and processing stage - the first half of the circuit responsible for amplifying the small input signals, rejecting common-mode noise, and filtering out interference. My partner designed the second half, which compared the processed signal against different voltage thresholds to drive the LED output.

The input stage faces several core challenges in EMG measurement:
